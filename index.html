Nondischargeable Debts in Bankruptcy

A plain-language guide to 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a) -- the 19 categories of debt that survive bankruptcy. Not every debt goes away when your case closes.

What Does Nondischargeable Mean?

Bankruptcy eliminates most debts -- but not all of them. Some debts survive your bankruptcy case and remain your legal obligation even after you receive a discharge. These are called nondischargeable debts.

Section 523(a) of the Bankruptcy Code lists 19 categories of debt that cannot be discharged. If a debt falls into one of these categories, the creditor can continue collecting after your case closes -- as if you never filed.

The nondischargeability rules apply in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13, though Chapter 13 historically offered a broader discharge (called the "superdischarge") that could wipe out some debts that Chapter 7 could not. That advantage was significantly narrowed in 2005.

Debts That Are Never Dischargeable

Several categories of debt are nondischargeable in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13. These are the most commonly encountered:

  • Domestic support obligations -- Section 523(a)(5). Child support and alimony are never dischargeable. This is the highest-priority nondischargeable debt in bankruptcy.
  • Certain tax debts -- Section 523(a)(1). Income taxes that are recent, unfiled, or fraudulently filed survive bankruptcy. Older taxes may be dischargeable if they meet specific timing rules (see below).
  • Debts obtained by fraud -- Section 523(a)(2)(A). If you obtained money, property, or services through false pretenses, false representation, or actual fraud, the debt survives. This includes credit card charges made with no intent to repay.
  • Willful and malicious injury -- Section 523(a)(6). Debts arising from intentional harm to another person or their property cannot be discharged. Negligence is not enough -- the injury must be deliberate.
  • Government fines and penalties -- Section 523(a)(7). Criminal fines, tax penalties, and other government-imposed penalties survive bankruptcy.
  • DUI debts -- Section 523(a)(9). Debts for death or personal injury caused by operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated are nondischargeable.

Other categories include debts not listed on the bankruptcy schedules (523(a)(3)), debts from embezzlement or larceny (523(a)(4)), debts to a spouse from a divorce decree (523(a)(15)), and condominium or co-op fees assessed after filing (523(a)(16)).

Student Loans -- Section 523(a)(8)

Student loan debt is nondischargeable unless the debtor can show that repayment would impose an undue hardship. For decades, courts applied the Brunner test -- a three-part standard requiring the debtor to show: (1) inability to maintain a minimal standard of living while repaying, (2) the hardship is likely to persist for a significant portion of the repayment period, and (3) good-faith efforts to repay.

The Brunner test has been widely criticized as too rigid. In recent years, courts have begun moving away from it. The First Circuit rejected Brunner entirely in 2023, and the Department of Justice issued revised guidance encouraging a more flexible, totality-of-the-circumstances approach.

The standard is evolving. If you have student loan debt, the current legal landscape is more favorable to discharge than it was even five years ago -- but it still requires filing an adversary proceeding and proving your case to the court.

Tax Debts -- When Taxes Become Dischargeable

Not all tax debts are nondischargeable. Income taxes can be discharged in bankruptcy if they meet all three timing requirements:

  • The 3-year rule -- the tax return was due (including extensions) at least 3 years before the bankruptcy filing.
  • The 2-year rule -- the tax return was actually filed at least 2 years before the bankruptcy filing.
  • The 240-day rule -- the tax was assessed at least 240 days before the bankruptcy filing.

If all three conditions are met, and the return was not fraudulent, the income tax debt may be dischargeable. Payroll taxes, trust fund taxes, and taxes where no return was ever filed are generally never dischargeable.

The Chapter 13 Superdischarge

Before 2005, Chapter 13 offered a significantly broader discharge than Chapter 7. Debts from fraud (523(a)(2)), embezzlement or larceny (523(a)(4)), and willful and malicious injury (523(a)(6)) were all dischargeable in Chapter 13 -- even though they survived Chapter 7. This was called the superdischarge.

The Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act (BAPCPA) of 2005 eliminated most of the superdischarge advantage. Today, Sections 523(a)(2), (a)(4), and (a)(6) are nondischargeable in both chapters.

The Chapter 13 superdischarge still exists in a narrower form. A handful of debt categories remain dischargeable in Chapter 13 but not in Chapter 7 -- most notably, debts arising from property settlements in divorce (523(a)(15)) and certain government fines. But the gap between the two chapters is much smaller than it used to be.

How to Challenge a Nondischargeability Claim

Not every debt a creditor claims is nondischargeable actually is. For certain exceptions -- specifically Sections 523(a)(2), (a)(4), and (a)(6) -- the creditor must file an adversary proceeding (a lawsuit within the bankruptcy case) to have the debt declared nondischargeable.

The deadline is critical: the creditor must file the complaint within 60 days after the first date set for the Section 341 meeting of creditors. If the creditor misses this deadline, the debt is discharged -- even if it would otherwise qualify as nondischargeable under those sections.

Other exceptions -- like domestic support obligations (523(a)(5)) and most tax debts (523(a)(1)) -- are automatically nondischargeable without any action by the creditor. No adversary proceeding is required.

If a creditor files a nondischargeability complaint against you, you have the right to defend. The creditor bears the burden of proof. Many adversary proceedings settle before trial, and some debtors successfully defeat nondischargeability claims entirely.
